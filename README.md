# Working With Me
A user manual on what it's like to work with me.

## Core Values

- **Empathy.** I put myself in other people's shoes. This often looks like me asking a lot about your experiences or perceptions so I can understand where you're coming from.
- **Reliability.** I'll always try to let you know in advance if I can't commit to something we agreed on. I hold others to this too. If in doubt, over-communicate.
- **Honesty.** If I don't know something, I'll tell you. Dishonesty degrades my trust and respect faster than anything. Just don't do it. Trust that even if something sounds bad, I'm going to try to understand your perspective first.

## How I Work

### Typical Day

- Start of workday at 9 AM
- Mornings: standing meetings, stand-ups
- Preference for collaborative meetings in the first half of the day
- Lunches are important and protected time
- Focused work in the afternoon
- Workday ends when tasks are complete

### I Do My Best Work When

- Goals are clear and I have autonomy in executing the work
- I have opportunities to get a pulse on how I'm doing
- I have a foundational understanding of what I'm working on
- I can collaborate with others

### What Drains Me

- Context-switching without transition time
- Last-minute changes that aren't well-communicated
- Unclear ownership, when nobody knows who's making the call
- Extended periods without feedback on how things are going

### Strengths

- I know a little about a lot of things and am enthusiastic to learn something new
- I adapt quickly to most situations, so long as it's clear what changes are taking place
- Empathy is the default. I always strive to understand different perspectives.

### Weaknesses

- I can fall into overthinking traps, which leads to decision paralysis
- I have a hard time speaking to large audiences. This is something I'm working on.

### How I Make Decisions

I need to understand the *why* before I commit. I'll ask a lot of questions, not to challenge, but to build enough context to move confidently. Once I'm in, I'm in. I'd rather align and commit than stall on disagreement. But I won't rubber-stamp something I don't understand.

## Interpersonal

### Feedback

**Receiving**

- High-value feedback:
  - Clear what the feedback is about
  - Actionable and realistic to act on
  - Delivered personally, one-on-one or async in an agreed-upon space
- Low-value feedback:
  - Indirect or non-specific
  - Attacks of character, accusations, assumptions
  - Delivered impersonally, like a random public channel or dropped into a large meeting

**Giving**

- I love helping others polish ideas, optimisations and improvements
- If you're soliciting my feedback, I'll ask what's most useful unless otherwise stated
- Feedback will be delivered personally unless otherwise stated

### Preferred Communication

**Synchronous**

- **Slack.** Best way to get my attention. I treat Slack as synchronous. If I can't actively participate, I'll let you know when I'll respond or schedule a follow-up.
- **Meetings.** If something can't be easily summarised in a written sentence, it's easier for me to discuss verbally. I may ask for a Slack Huddle or Google Hangout.

**Asynchronous**

- **Figma / Loom Comments.** Best for design-specific feedback.
- **Documents.** Best for big ideas that require context and framing. I prefer Notion, but Google Docs works.
- **Email.** Best way to communicate *non-urgent* things to me.

Most times I have Granola AI running. My preference is recorded meetings. AI gets things wrong sometimes.

### Conflict Resolution

I'm conflict-averse as a default, but I'm not afraid to state my concerns when necessary. Conflict is most likely to come up for me when there's perceived injustice or when facts are intentionally obscured.

If we're in conflict, talk to me directly. I'm going to try to understand where you're coming from rather than make assumptions. When I ask a lot of questions, it's not an attack. It's how I process.

### Support

- **Receiving.** I will always try to find an answer before asking a question. Clear, directive support is important to me.
- **Providing.** I'm often the first to jump at the chance to help in ways I can contribute meaningfully.

### As a Manager

**How I lead**

- I give you space to own your work, but I'm always there when you need me.
- I'll be direct with feedback. I'd rather you hear it clearly from me than have to guess.
- Your growth matters to me. I'll push you toward development opportunities and check in on where you want to go.
- I'll advocate for your time, priorities, and recognition. If something needs to be shielded, I'll handle it.

**What I expect from you**

- Own your work. Take initiative, don't wait to be told what to do.
- Communicate proactively. Surface blockers early. Over-communicate rather than under.
- Help me meet you where you are. Be honest about what you know and where you'd like to develop. It helps me anticipate ways to support you better.
- Bring solutions. Flag problems, but come with ideas on how to fix them. If you don't know, tell me how I can best help you.

### What I Need From My Manager

- Clear expectations and regular check-ins on how I'm tracking
- Autonomy in execution once direction is set
- Honest, direct feedback. Don't sugarcoat it.
- Air cover when I need to push back on scope or priorities

### Common Misunderstandings About Me

- My questions can come off as defensive. It's an effort to gain clarification, not a rejection of feedback.
- I can seem rigid in communication. I favor structure when working through abstract or difficult ideas.

## Product & Design Principles

- **Validate early and often.** Ideas are fleeting. Stay nimble. Don't let perfection stand in the way of progress.
- **Turn constraints into opportunities.** Understand the boundaries of the environments you're designing in, their gaps, and opportunity areas.
- **Embody being data-driven.** Do the work to understand data, its nuances, and how it drives decisions.
- **Aim for simplicity.** When introducing new patterns, consider what you're requiring the customer to learn.
- **Commit, even if you disagree.** Healthy disagreement is fine. At a minimum, align and commit to the outcomes.

## Working With Teams

- **Respect your experts.** Subject matter experts are gold. Trust their expertise and learn from them.
- **Set expectations early, explicitly.** Avoid misunderstandings by aligning as early and clearly as possible.
- **Be a team advocate.** Ensure your team has everything they need to do their best work.
- **Always be evangelising.** Champion your best ideas by sharing them and educating others at both the team and company levels.
